Turbo Jet Engine

 Project Description:

 This project was inspired by famous youtuber Colin Furze and his various turbo jet projects. The turbo jet in this project consists of a turbocharger, combustion chamber, and oil system. The turbocharger was found at a local junk yard, the oil system was constructed using common plumbing materials and the combustion chamber was constructed with 1/16 inch thick 304 stainless steel. Propane was used as an atomized propellant. The high-level design and function of each of these components is detailed below:

 TurboCharger

 When starting this project, the first item that should be acquired is the turbocharger itself. This is because the geometry of the combustion chamber depends on the inlet diameter of the turbojet (this is described in the video linked in the combustion chamber section). The turbocharger's function is to intake air through the intake turbine, send it through the combustion chamber and expel the exhaust gases out the exhaust turbine. Both the intake and exhaust turbines are directly connected via a turboshaft. Ideally, a turbo jet of this construction should be able to self sustain the rotation of both turbines after being primed with a leaf blower. Once primed, the exhaust gases generated from the continuous combustion process should be enough to perpetuate the rotation of the exit turbine.
 Combustion Chamber

 This video acted as a guide for most of the design and manufacturing decisions of the combustion chamber. The function of the combustion chamber is to direct intake air and fuel into a chamber where they mix in a vortex fashion. Once mixed, a sparkplug or match ignites the mixture and continuous combustion takes place. The exhaust gases are then directed out the exit turbine and thrust is generated.
 Oil System

 The oil system consists of an oil reservoir, pump, electronic dial controller, small motorcycle battery, blowoff valve, needle valve, and pressure gauge. Oil is pumped from the reservoir to the turboshaft and then recycled back into the reservoir. The oil serves two purposes; lubrication and coolant of the turboshaft. A rough sketch of the oil system is shown in the manufacturing process gallery. To control oil pressure, the electronic dial controller can be used to adjust the voltage being sent to the pump and pressure can be read by the pressure gauge. This can be adjusted until the desired pressure is reached (~30psi gauge pressure). The blowoff valve serves as the first safety feature and will open to let oil back into the reservoir if pressure exceeds a certain limit. The needle valve acts as a redundant safety feature and can be manually adjusted to let oil back into the reservoir if pressure exceeds a desired limit.

 Future Work:

  As shown in the video below, the turbo jet functioned properly when provided intake air from a leaf blower. When the leaf blower was removed, the turboshaft stopped spinning. Ideally, the continuous combustion process should perpetuate the spinning of the turboshaft once primed with a leaf blower. It was determined that the turboshaft had too much friction from a buildup of tar-like burnt oil from before it was acquired for this project. Future work should entail cleaning this gunk from the turboshaft to reduce the amount of friction when spinning. It is also hypothesized that a nozzle would benefit the operation of the turbo jet.
