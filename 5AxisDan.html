5-Axis 3D Printer

 Project Description:

 This project was inspired by Dan Brogan's senior Mechanical Engineering capstone project at the University of Rhode Island. Before the COVID-19 pandemic restricted their access to campus, Dan's team had designed and constructed a 3D printer only capable of following a 3-axis toolpath. While they had created a machine that is physically capable of 5-axis motion, they did not create a software solution to following a 5-axis toolpath.
 After graduating, Dan Brogan decided to design and build his own 5-axis 3D printer. Dan invented a software solution capable of converting 3-axis gcode for certain axisymmetric parts to be converted to 5-axis gcode. While his solution is currently limited to certain part geometries, he believes there is potential for his methodology to create 5-axis instructions for more complex geometries in the future.

 5-Axis Toolpath Demonstration:

 The toolpath demonstration video below shows a 5-axis toolpath of a dumbell-shaped part. The part was modelled in Autodesk Inventor and sliced using Ultimaker Cura. The 3-axis gcode from Cura was then interpreted by Dan's software and converted to 5-axis instructions. The video shows a complete 5-axis print routine where the extruder centers over the build platform, prints the 3-axis component of the part including a substrate for when the other two axes are activated, rotates the build plate 90 degrees and begins printing in 5-axis mode.



 Design Features:

 An important feature of this 3D printer (referred to as the "Forge Vc", c for compact) is its manufacturability. Most parts on the Forge Vc are standard mass-produced parts found on traditional 3-axis printers. With the exception of the printing bed cradle assembly, most custom components of the Forge Vc can be printed on a 3-axis printer. If a part breaks on the Forge Vc, it can likely be repaired quickly if another 3D printer is nearby. A competetive feature of the Forge Vc is that no complex 5-axis slicing software is needed. For compatible geometries, the Forge Vc simply takes 3-axis gcode as input and generates its own 5-axis instructions based on the machine's capabilities. Right now the Forge Vc can print any traditional 3-axis part in 3-axis mode and can act as an additive lathe in 5-axis mode. Future work should investigate how to expand its 5-axis capabilities past certain axisymmetric parts.
